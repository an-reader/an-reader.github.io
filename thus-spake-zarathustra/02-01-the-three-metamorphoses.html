[4] Three metamorphoses of the spirit do I designate to you: how the spirit becometh a camel, the camel a lion, and the lion at last a child.

[10] What is the heaviest thing, ye heroes? asketh the load-bearing spirit, that I may take it upon me and rejoice in my strength.

[16] Or is it this: To feed on the acorns and grass of knowledge, and for the sake of truth to suffer hunger of soul?

[20] Or is it this: To go into foul water when it is the water of truth, and not disclaim cold frogs and hot toads?

[22] Or is it this: To love those who despise us, and give one’s hand to the phantom when it is going to frighten us?

[24] All these heaviest things the load-bearing spirit taketh upon itself: and like the camel, which, when laden, hasteneth into the wilderness, so hasteneth the spirit into its wilderness.

[26] But in the loneliest wilderness happeneth the second metamorphosis: here the spirit becometh a lion; freedom will it capture, and lordship in its own wilderness.

[28] Its last Lord it here seeketh: hostile will it be to him, and to its last God; for victory will it struggle with the great dragon.

[30] What is the great dragon which the spirit is no longer inclined to call Lord and God? “Thou shalt,” is the great dragon called. But the spirit of the lion saith, “I will.”

[32] “Thou shalt,” lieth in its path, sparkling with gold—a scale-covered beast; and on every scale glittereth golden, “Thou shalt!”

[34] The values of a thousand years glitter on those scales, and thus speaketh the mightiest of all dragons: “All the values of things—glitter on me.

[36] All values have already been created, and all created values—do I represent. Verily, there shall be no ‘I will’ any more.” Thus speaketh the dragon.
